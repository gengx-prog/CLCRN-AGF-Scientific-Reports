\title{Adaptive Graph Fusion for Conditional Local Convolution: A Schedule-Matched, Multi-Seed Evaluation on WeatherBench}

\author[1,*]{Xinchen Geng}
\author[1]{Hung-Yu Lin}
\affil[1]{University of Southern California, Los Angeles, CA 90089, USA}
\affil[*]{Corresponding author: gengx@usc.edu}

\keywords{Weather forecasting; Spatio-temporal graph neural networks; Adaptive graph learning; Spherical grids; Reproducibility}

\begin{abstract}
Conditional Local Convolution Recurrent Network (CLCRN) is an established
graph-recurrent model for meteorological forecasting. Rather than pursuing a
leaderboard result, we ask whether augmenting this backbone with a compact
adaptive graph-fusion (AGF) encoder---which learns non-local node dependencies
before the unchanged recurrent backbone---yields reproducible,
architecture-attributable gains, and for which variables. The extension builds
a directed adjacency from trainable source and destination node embeddings,
keeps a renormalized top-$k$ neighbourhood, and fuses the graph-aggregated
representation with a residual projection through a learned gate. Using the
WeatherBench preprocessing and four tasks from the original CLCRN study (2-m
temperature, relative humidity, 10-m surface wind, and total cloud cover), we
compare CLCRN-AGF with a schedule-matched CLCRN control over five seed
replicates. The clearest effect is on relative humidity, where CLCRN-AGF
reduces mean absolute error by 2.28\% and root mean squared error by 3.02\%,
improving in every replicate and surviving multiple-comparison correction.
Effects on temperature, wind, and cloud cover are small or metric-dependent,
and component ablations attribute the humidity gain chiefly to the adaptive
graph branch. The study shows how schedule-matched controls, multi-seed
replication, and significance testing separate genuine architectural gains
from optimization and reporting effects.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Numerical weather prediction (NWP) remains the operational standard for
atmospheric forecasting because it explicitly integrates the governing
physical equations~\cite{bauer2015quiet}. Its accuracy, however, comes at a
substantial computational cost, and recent years have seen data-driven models
emerge as fast and increasingly capable alternatives for selected forecasting
tasks. The release of WeatherBench established a common benchmark built on the ERA5
reanalysis that made systematic comparison of such models
possible~\cite{rasp2020weatherbench,hersbach2020era5},
and early convolutional sequence models on regular or cubed-sphere grids
demonstrated that deep networks can learn skillful short-range
dynamics~\cite{weyn2020improving}. At the large-model end of the spectrum,
FourCastNet, Pangu-Weather, GraphCast, ClimaX, spherical Fourier neural
operators, neural general circulation models, and probabilistic diffusion
forecasters now rival or exceed operational NWP at global
scale~\cite{pathak2022fourcastnet,bi2023accurate,lam2023learning,nguyen2023climax,bonev2023spherical,kochkov2024neural,price2025probabilistic}.
Smaller graph-recurrent systems nonetheless remain relevant whenever
retraining cost, task-specific adaptation, controlled ablation, and
reproducibility matter more than raw global skill.

\caption{\textbf{Motivation: forecasting on the Earth's curved, non-Euclidean
manifold.} Left: on the spherical surface, geodesic distance, relative

convolution kernel shared across the grid is geometrically mismatched.
Right: a graph model built on the curved mesh must adapt the scale and

compact local support (bottom), driving geometric overfitting from high to
low. This motivates the geometry-conditioned CLConv backbone retained in
this study, and raises the question of what a learned non-local graph adds
on top of it.}
\label{fig:background}
\end{figure}

Because the atmosphere is sampled on an irregular spherical grid rather than a
Euclidean image lattice (Fig.~\ref{fig:background}), graph neural networks
(GNNs) are a natural inductive bias for these data. On the curved manifold,
geodesic distance, relative orientation, and spatial scale all vary across the
globe, so a single shared convolution kernel is geometrically mismatched and
geometry-unaware models are prone to overfitting; geometry-conditioned graph
convolution with appropriate regularization is therefore better suited to the
data. Directly relevant to the present study, the Conditional Local Convolution
Recurrent Network (CLCRN)~\cite{lin2022conditional} introduced a Conditional
Local Convolution (CLConv) that generates \emph{location-dependent} kernels
from spherical coordinates, geodesic distance, and directional information,
thereby addressing the spatially shared-kernel limitation of conventional
graph convolution on an unstructured spherical grid. We review the broader
spatio-temporal graph, attention-based, and weather-forecasting literature in
Sections~\ref{sec:rw-stgnn}--\ref{sec:rw-weather}.

Despite this progress, an open question persists for geometry-aware backbones
such as CLCRN. CLConv already encodes geographically local structure through
its coordinate-conditioned kernels, yet many meteorological variables exhibit
dependencies that are not fully described by spatial proximity—moisture
transport, large-scale circulation, and teleconnections couple regions that
are geodesically distant. It is therefore unclear whether adding a learned,
\emph{non-local} adaptive graph on top of a backbone that is already
geometry-conditioned yields genuine and reproducible gains, or merely adds
parameters. Two confounds make this hard to answer in practice. First,
reported improvements often entangle architectural changes with optimization
and software-environment differences, so single best-run comparisons can be
misleading. Second, gains can be task-specific, and aggregate averages hide
which variables actually benefit. Resolving these issues requires a
schedule-matched control, multiple random-seed replicates with explicit
significance testing, and a clean separation of published reference values
from newly generated results—an evaluation discipline that the recent
benchmarking literature increasingly emphasizes.

To address this question we propose \textbf{CLCRN-AGF} (Fig.~\ref{fig:overview}),
which inserts a compact adaptive graph-fusion encoder before the original
CLCRN recurrent backbone, leaving the backbone unchanged. Trainable source and
destination node embeddings define a directed similarity matrix; a sparse
top-$k$ neighbourhood aggregates node features; and a learned gate mixes the
graph-aggregated representation with a residual projection. The design tests
the focused hypothesis that non-local learned dependencies add value beyond
CLCRN's geographically conditioned local convolution, particularly for
variables whose dynamics are not fully described by local proximity. The study
is deliberately framed as a controlled evaluation and reproducibility analysis
rather than a leaderboard entry, and its primary contribution is methodological.

The main contributions of this paper are as follows:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Controlled Evaluation Protocol.} A schedule-matched control,
five-seed replication, confidence intervals, Welch tests,
false-discovery-rate correction, and a distribution-free check that together
separate architectural effects from optimization and reporting effects.
\item \textbf{Adaptive Graph-Fusion (AGF) Encoder.} The compact adaptive
top-$k$ graph-fusion encoder that is the object of this evaluation, attached
to the established CLCRN backbone while leaving the backbone unchanged.
\item \textbf{Component Ablations and Diagnostics.} Ablations separating the
adaptive graph branch from the learned fusion gate, together with
gate-activation, robustness, and convergence diagnostics.
\item \textbf{Transparent Reporting.} A transparent separation between
published reference values, independent reproduction, and newly generated
results.
\item \textbf{A Positive as well as Negative Result.} The extension
consistently helps relative humidity but gives small or metric-dependent
changes for the other tasks.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Spatio-temporal graph neural networks: from fixed to learned topology}
\label{sec:rw-stgnn}
Three strands of prior work frame the design summarized in
Fig.~\ref{fig:overview}. The first supplies the graph-convolutional machinery
itself: spectral and spatial graph
convolutions~\cite{defferrard2016chebnet,kipf2017semi}, graph
attention~\cite{velickovic2018graph}, and inductive neighborhood
aggregation~\cite{hamilton2017inductive} established the basic operator
toolbox, while gauge-equivariant convolutions designed for icosahedral and
spherical meshes~\cite{cohen2019gauge} brought that toolbox to the curved
domains that weather data inhabit.

Building on these operators, spatio-temporal GNNs (STGNNs) for structured
forecasting initially consumed a fixed, pre-computed topology---diffusion
convolution within recurrent encoders~\cite{li2018diffusion}, fully
convolutional spatio-temporal graphs~\cite{yu2018spatio}, and
attention-augmented graph convolution~\cite{guo2019attention} all operate on
distance-thresholded geographic graphs of the kind shown in
Fig.~\ref{fig:overview}c (binary and distance-weighted adjacency). A second
wave learned the topology itself: self-adaptive
adjacency~\cite{wu2019graph}, node-adaptive parameter
learning~\cite{bai2020adaptive}, jointly learned mix-hop
graphs~\cite{wu2020connecting}, multi-attention networks~\cite{zheng2020gman},
continuous-time graph ODEs~\cite{fang2021spatial}, and dynamic
spatial--temporal aware graphs~\cite{lan2022dstagnn} infer edges from
trainable node embeddings, the lineage that the adaptive attention graph in
Fig.~\ref{fig:overview}c follows. Comprehensive surveys document this
trajectory~\cite{jin2023spatio}, and recent work extends graph forecasting to
scalable, probabilistic, state-space, and irregularly-sampled
settings~\cite{cini2023scalable,zambon2023graph,wen2023diffstg,oskarsson2023temporal}.
Most of these STGNNs, however, assume a Euclidean or generic graph domain;
geometry-conditioned operators such as CLConv
(Fig.~\ref{fig:overview}b) remain comparatively rare, which motivates
retaining CLCRN as the fixed, unmodified backbone of this study.

\subsection{Attention-based and long-sequence forecasting models}
\label{sec:rw-attention}
A second strand removes the pre-specified graph altogether and lets attention
or probabilistic structure learning discover the dependency pattern. Examples
include discrete graph-structure inference~\cite{shang2021discrete}, position-
and edge-conditioned processing on point
clouds~\cite{qi2017pointnet,wang2019dynamic}, and propagation-delay-aware or
adaptive-embedding transformers for spatio-temporal
data~\cite{jiang2023pdformer,liu2023spatio,feng2022adaptive,huang2023adaptive}.
General-purpose long-sequence forecasters point the same way: the
Transformer~\cite{vaswani2017attention} and its forecasting descendants
Informer~\cite{zhou2021informer}, Autoformer~\cite{wu2021autoformer},
PatchTST~\cite{nie2023time}, TimesNet~\cite{wu2023timesnet},
Crossformer~\cite{zhang2023crossformer}, and
iTransformer~\cite{liu2024itransformer}, alongside selective state-space
models~\cite{gu2023mamba}, spectral graph forecasters~\cite{cao2020spectral},
pure deep stacks such as N-BEATS~\cite{oreshkin2020nbeats}, and the
deliberately simple DLinear~\cite{zeng2023transformers}, repeatedly show that
learned, non-local dependency structure can substitute for a pre-specified
topology. This is precisely the hypothesis that the gated fusion of local and
graph representations in Fig.~\ref{fig:overview}a is designed to test on a
geometry-conditioned backbone: if non-local learned structure carries signal
that the local CLConv kernel misses, the gate has room to exploit it.

\subsection{Deep learning for weather and climate prediction}
\label{sec:rw-weather}
The third strand is the application domain itself. Within
\textit{Scientific Reports}, adaptive and multi-scale spatio-temporal graph
networks have become a recurring theme for traffic and environmental
forecasting~\cite{chen2025multiscale,xiao2025adaptive,xiao2025graph}, as have
deep models for climate and microclimate
prediction~\cite{frnda2022ecmwf,zanchi2023microclimate,guo2024monthly,alves2025spatiotemporal}.
The present study follows this trajectory but inverts the usual emphasis:
rather than proposing a larger model, it treats the geometry-conditioned
CLCRN backbone of Fig.~\ref{fig:overview}b as a fixed substrate and asks,
under a controlled, schedule-matched, multi-seed protocol, whether the
learned non-local graph of Fig.~\ref{fig:overview}c adds reproducible,
architecture-attributable value.

\section{Results}

\subsection{Experimental setup}
\subsubsection{Datasets}
We evaluate on the four WeatherBench-derived tasks defined
by the original CLCRN study~\cite{lin2022conditional,rasp2020weatherbench}:
2-m temperature (K), relative humidity (percentage points), 10-m surface wind
components ($u,v$; m\,s$^{-1}$), and total cloud cover (fraction). Hourly ERA5
records from 1~January 2010 to 31~December 2018 are mapped to a $32\times64$
latitude--longitude grid with $N=2{,}048$ nodes. Each example uses 12
consecutive hourly observations to forecast the next 12 hours; starting
windows are sampled once per day, yielding 3,286 examples split
chronologically into 70\%/10\%/20\% for train/validation/test.

\subsubsection{Baselines and comparison protocol}
The primary, causal comparison is
between CLCRN-AGF and a \emph{schedule-matched control}: the original CLCRN
backbone trained with the identical data, optimizer, training budget, and
random seeds but with the AGF module disabled. As external context we also
report (i) persistence on the current split; (ii) the published CLCRN
reference values~\cite{lin2022conditional}; (iii) a single independent
reproduction of the public CLCRN implementation; and (iv) the family of graph
forecasting baselines benchmarked in the original CLCRN study—STGCN~\cite{yu2018spatio},
DCRNN~\cite{li2018diffusion}, AGCRN~\cite{bai2020adaptive},
ASTGCN and MSTGCN~\cite{guo2019attention}, a graph-convolutional
GRU~\cite{seo2018structured}, and a temporal GCN~\cite{zhao2019tgcn}—whose
relative standing against the CLCRN backbone is summarized
in Fig.~\ref{fig:baselines}. These external numbers combine architecture,
software, and protocol effects and are therefore used only to situate the
backbone, not to attribute the AGF effect.

\subsubsection{Evaluation metrics and significance}
We report mean absolute error (MAE) and
root mean squared error (RMSE) computed after inverse standardization, using a
corrected zero-inclusive evaluator (zero wind and zero cloud cover are
physically meaningful and are not treated as missing). The primary analysis
uses five random-seed replicates $\{2021,\dots,2025\}$; for each metric we
report the mean and standard deviation across seeds, the mean difference, a
two-sided 95\% Welch confidence interval, and a two-sided Welch test.

\subsubsection{Implementation details}
Both variants use two recurrent layers with 32 hidden
units; CLCRN-AGF adds $d_e=10$-dimensional adaptive source/destination node
embeddings, an 8-dimensional AGF output, and $k=4$ selected neighbours
(self-edge included).
Training uses Adam (learning rate 0.01, batch size 32, gradient clipping 5)
with a MultiStepLR schedule for up to 100 epochs, selecting the
lowest-validation-MAE checkpoint. All runs use Python 3.13, PyTorch 2.10,
CUDA 12.8, and a single NVIDIA GeForce RTX 5090 Laptop GPU. Full
hyperparameters are listed in Methods.

Before evaluating the extension, Fig.~\ref{fig:baselines} situates the CLCRN
backbone relative to seven graph forecasting baselines reported in the
original CLCRN benchmark, across all four variables and both metrics. The
backbone is consistently strong, which is the reason it is adopted here as a
fixed substrate: the present study does not claim CLCRN or CLConv as new
contributions, and these external values are shown only as context for the

\subsection{Statistical significance analysis}
Because eight task/metric comparisons are evaluated jointly, we control the
false discovery rate across them with the Benjamini--Hochberg procedure. Only
the two relative-humidity improvements remain significant after correction
(adjusted $p=6.7\times10^{-4}$ for RMSE and $3.0\times10^{-3}$ for MAE), while
every other comparison---including the temperature RMSE difference (adjusted
$p=0.25$)---is not significant (adjusted $p\ge0.25$). As a distribution-free
check that does not rely on the normality assumption at $n=5$, a one-sided sign
test on the humidity replicates---all five seeds favour CLCRN-AGF for both
metrics---gives $p=0.031$. The humidity effect is therefore the only one that
survives both multiple-comparison correction and a non-parametric test. These
results demonstrate that the observed humidity gain is not caused by random
initialization.

\subsection{Per-step error growth}
Figure~\ref{fig:horizon} reports how exact and cumulative error grow with the
forecast horizon for each task. As expected for short-range forecasting, error
increases monotonically from $t{+}1$ to $t{+}12$, but the shape of the growth is
variable-specific: temperature and wind accumulate error close to linearly,
humidity follows a concave-up profile driven by nonlinear moisture transport,
and cloud cover grows sub-linearly beyond roughly $t{+}4$, consistent with
semi-periodic cloud cycles. This profile indicates that the humidity advantage
in Table~\ref{tab:primary} is not an artifact of a single lead time but accrues
across the horizon where errors are largest.

\subsection{Component ablations}
The experiment archive also contains component ablations. Table~\ref{tab:ablation}
separates three kinds of evidence: 100-epoch, three-seed ablations for
temperature and relative humidity, and a 20-epoch single-seed screening run
over all four tasks. The full-budget humidity comparison uses seeds
2023--2025. The w/o gated fusion humidity completion was trained with
automatic mixed precision after repeated standard-precision runs exhausted
the local CUDA allocator; checkpoint selection and test evaluation still use
the same corrected zero-inclusive evaluator.

In the 100-epoch temperature ablation, removing the entire adaptive graph
branch worsens RMSE relative to the full model, while replacing the learned
gate by mean fusion gives similar or slightly lower mean errors. Thus the
gate is not uniformly beneficial for temperature. In the full-budget humidity
ablation, both adaptive-graph variants improve on the model without the
adaptive graph branch. The full gated model has the lowest mean MAE and RMSE,
but the mean-fusion variant remains close and was completed under the
mixed-precision caveat described above. We therefore treat this block as
sensitivity evidence that supports adaptive graph mixing, not as a definitive
precision-matched test of the gate. The 20-epoch screening
run reaches the same qualitative warning: relative humidity is the most
sensitive task, whereas wind, temperature, and cloud-cover screening results
are mixed.

\subsection{Decomposing the improvement: schedule versus structure}
A central reproducibility concern is that apparent architectural gains may in
fact originate from the training schedule. Figure~\ref{fig:decomp} decomposes
the test MAE of four configurations—an original reproduction, the
schedule-matched control (revised schedule, no adaptive branch), the full
CLCRN-AGF model, and the published paper target. The revised schedule alone
accounts for most of the improvement on wind and cloud cover, with the
additional AGF contribution being small; for humidity, the adaptive branch adds
a further, consistent reduction on top of the schedule. This decomposition is
the reason the five-replicate, schedule-matched control—rather than a single
best run—is treated as the primary causal comparison throughout.

\subsection{Reproducibility, stability, and convergence}
Because the schedule-matched comparison is the basis of our conclusions, we
characterize its run-to-run stability directly. Across the five seeds in
Table~\ref{tab:primary}, CLCRN-AGF is highly repeatable: the per-seed standard
deviation of test MAE is at most $0.032$ (temperature) and is much smaller for
the other variables ($0.013$ for humidity, $0.007$ for wind, and $0.0013$ for
cloud cover), with RMSE dispersion bounded by $0.047$. Temperature is the most
seed-sensitive variable---consistent with its smoother, more isotropic spatial
field, for which optimization noise has a relatively larger influence on the
final error---whereas humidity, wind, and cloud cover converge to nearly
identical solutions once the schedule is fixed. The five-seed means also track
the published CLCRN reference closely for humidity and cloud cover, indicating
that the reported operating point is representative rather than a favourable
single run.

Convergence behaviour reinforces the need for the longer schedule. Under the
100-epoch budget, the best validation checkpoint is reached on average at epoch
$78$ (temperature), $87$ (humidity), $86$ (wind), and $93$ (cloud cover), and
in several seeds approaches the budget ceiling. A short schedule would
therefore systematically under-train the model and blur the separation between
optimization and architectural effects that Fig.~\ref{fig:decomp} is designed
to expose; humidity additionally shows the widest seed-to-seed spread in
stopping epoch, so convergence speed is itself task-dependent.

\subsection{Robustness and computational cost}
To probe robustness, Fig.~\ref{fig:robust} measures degradation under
increasing missing-node ratios at inference, simulating sensor dropout on the
spherical grid. Cloud cover is essentially insensitive, wind degrades slowly,
and temperature and humidity degrade more steeply, mirroring the per-task error
magnitudes in Fig.~\ref{fig:horizon}. On cost, the AGF encoder approximately
doubles the trainable parameters (from 39.3k to 80.9k for temperature) and
increases peak inference memory (from 352 to 635~MB) because it forms a dense
$N\times N$ similarity matrix before top-$k$ selection, while per-batch
inference latency rises only marginally (202.6 to 211.9~ms). The extension is
compact relative to global foundation models, but it is not cost-free and is
not asymptotically linear in the number of nodes (Methods).

\subsection{Gate-activation diagnostics}
The fusion gate $\mathbf{g}\in(0,1)$ controls, per node and channel, the
balance between the graph-aggregated representation $\mathbf{q}$ and the
residual/local representation $\mathbf{r}$ ($\mathbf{z}=\mathbf{g}\odot\mathbf{q}+(1-\mathbf{g})\odot\mathbf{r}$).
Table~\ref{tab:gate} summarizes the learned gate values over the full test set,
and Figure~\ref{fig:gate} shows their distribution (gate activations were
exported for the three non-temperature variables). For each of these variables
the mean gate is close to $0.5$ (humidity $\mu=0.55$, wind $\mu=0.47$, cloud
cover $\mu=0.45$), so the model does not collapse onto either pathway; it uses
both the non-local graph and the local residual. Humidity places slightly more
weight on the graph branch and has the most concentrated distribution
($\sigma=0.043$), consistent with its being the variable that benefits most
from the adaptive graph; cloud cover has the widest spread ($\sigma=0.106$),
indicating a more sample-dependent balance. We report these statistics only as
internal diagnostics: a non-degenerate gate distribution is consistent with,
but does not by itself prove, a physical interpretation of either pathway.

\section{Discussion}

\paragraph{What does the replicate experiment establish?}
The replicate experiment supports a narrow conclusion. Adding adaptive graph
fusion to CLCRN is beneficial for relative humidity under the tested setup:
the improvement is consistent across all five seed labels and the Welch
confidence interval excludes zero for both MAE and RMSE. For temperature,
wind, and cloud cover, the evidence does not support a general claim of
architectural superiority.

\paragraph{Why does relative humidity benefit the most?}
This task dependence is plausible but should not be over-interpreted.
Relative humidity may benefit from dependencies that are not captured by the
fixed local neighbourhood alone, which is consistent with the long-range and
adaptive-graph motivations in the broader spatio-temporal forecasting
literature~\cite{wu2019graph,bai2020adaptive,shang2021discrete,chen2025multiscale,xiao2025adaptive}.
The current experiment does not, however, demonstrate that the learned edges
correspond to teleconnections or moisture corridors. Establishing such a claim
would require spatial edge analysis, comparisons with meteorological indices,
and perturbation tests.

\paragraph{Separating optimization from architectural effects.}
The study also separates optimization effects from architectural effects.
The revised schedule substantially improves wind and cloud-cover results even
without AGF, while the additional AGF gain is small (Fig.~\ref{fig:decomp}).
Reporting only the best single run would obscure this distinction. The
five-replicate design is therefore central to the present conclusions. The
ablations further show that the two AGF components do not contribute uniformly.
For humidity, removing the adaptive graph branch gives the weakest full-budget
result, whereas mean fusion without the learned gate remains close to the full
model. For temperature, mean fusion can also match or outperform the learned
gate. The evidence therefore supports the adaptive graph branch more strongly
than it supports a universal benefit from gated fusion, although the humidity
gate comparison should be interpreted cautiously because one ablation variant
used mixed-precision training.

\paragraph{Limitations and future work.}
Several limitations remain. First, the study inherits the compact
single-variable setup of the original CLCRN paper rather than evaluating
full-state medium-range weather prediction as in recent global
forecasters~\cite{bi2023accurate,lam2023learning,kochkov2024neural,price2025probabilistic}.
Second, the current baseline set contains persistence, the published CLCRN
reference, and a schedule-matched control; modern graph and non-graph
baselines~\cite{liu2023spatio,nie2023time,liu2024itransformer,gu2023mamba}
still need to be retrained under the identical data and optimization pipeline.
Third, the top-$k$ graph is obtained from a dense $N\times N$ similarity
matrix, giving quadratic intermediate cost; sparse or linear-attention
formulations are a natural remedy. Fourth, full-budget component ablations are
now available for temperature and relative humidity, but wind and cloud cover
still rely on the shorter 20-epoch component screening and should not be
over-interpreted. The humidity mean-fusion completion used mixed-precision
training, so a fully precision-matched ablation grid would be useful before
making stronger claims about the gate.

\section{Conclusion}

This work presents a controlled evaluation of an adaptive graph-fusion
extension to the published CLCRN weather-forecasting model, with the central
aim of measuring---rather than asserting---when such an extension helps. Through
a schedule-matched control, five-seed replication, and significance testing,
the manuscript cleanly distinguishes prior CLCRN contributions, external
published values, independent reproduction, and new experiments. Under this
protocol, CLCRN-AGF consistently improves relative-humidity forecasting, while
changes for temperature, wind, and cloud cover are small or metric-dependent,
and a schedule-versus-structure decomposition shows that much of the apparent
gain over the published reference is attributable to the optimized training
schedule rather than the architecture. The results therefore support adaptive
graph fusion as a task-dependent extension rather than a universally better
replacement for the original CLCRN encoder. Beyond this specific model, the
study's main takeaway is methodological: schedule-matched, multi-seed,
significance-tested evaluation is a low-cost discipline that separates genuine
architectural gains from optimization and reporting effects, and is directly
transferable to other spatio-temporal forecasting systems.

\section{Methods}

\subsection{Data and preprocessing}
We use the same four WeatherBench-derived tasks and public preprocessing
pipeline as the original CLCRN study~\cite{lin2022conditional,rasp2020weatherbench}:
2-m temperature (K), relative humidity (percentage points), 10-m surface wind
components ($u$ and $v$, m\,s$^{-1}$), and total cloud cover (fraction).
Hourly ERA5 records from 1 January 2010 through 31 December 2018 are mapped to
a $32\times64$ latitude--longitude grid with $N=2{,}048$ nodes.
We denote the spherical grid by
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{V}=\{(\lambda_i,\phi_i)\}_{i=1}^{N},\qquad N=32\times64=2{,}048,
\label{eq:grid}
\end{equation}
where $\lambda_i$ and $\phi_i$ are longitude and latitude.

Each example contains 12 consecutive hourly observations and targets the next
12 hours. Starting windows are sampled once per day (step size 24), producing
3,286 examples. The split is chronological and unshuffled: 2,300 training
examples (70\%), 329 validation examples (10\%), and 657 test examples
(20\%). Input and target variables are standardized with means and
standard deviations computed from the training subset only; metrics are
calculated after inverse transformation.
For a starting index $s$, the input--target pair is
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{X}^{(s)}=\big[\mathbf{x}_{s},\ldots,\mathbf{x}_{s+T-1}\big],\qquad
\mathbf{Y}^{(s)}=\big[\mathbf{x}_{s+T},\ldots,\mathbf{x}_{s+T+H-1}\big],
\quad T=H=12,
\label{eq:window}
\end{equation}
with $\mathbf{x}_t\in\R^{N\times C}$. Standardization uses training statistics,
\begin{equation}
\bar{\mathbf{x}}_{t}
=\big(\mathbf{x}_{t}-\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\mathrm{train}}\big)
\oslash\boldsymbol{\sigma}_{\mathrm{train}},
\label{eq:standardization}
\end{equation}
where $\oslash$ denotes element-wise division.

\subsection{CLCRN backbone}
The backbone is the public CLCRN implementation of Lin et
al.~\cite{lin2022conditional}. CLConv generates a location-dependent kernel
from each centre--neighbour coordinate pair and multiplies it by
geodesic-distance and orientation factors. The resulting operator replaces the
linear transforms in a graph-convolutional GRU and is used in a
sequence-to-sequence encoder--decoder over $T=12$ input steps and $H=12$
forecast steps with autoregressive teacher forcing. We retain this backbone
without claiming its architecture as a contribution of the present study.
For node $i$ and neighbour $j$, the geometric descriptor can be written as
\begin{equation}
\boldsymbol{\xi}_{ij}
=\big[\lambda_i,\phi_i,\lambda_j,\phi_j,d_{ij},\theta_{ij}\big],
\label{eq:geometry_descriptor}
\end{equation}
where $d_{ij}$ is geodesic distance and $\theta_{ij}$ is relative orientation.
The local CLConv kernel is therefore location dependent,
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{K}_{ij}^{(\ell)}
=f_{\ell}\!\left(\boldsymbol{\xi}_{ij}\right)
\rho_{d}(d_{ij})\,\rho_{\theta}(\theta_{ij}),
\label{eq:clconv_kernel}
\end{equation}
where $f_{\ell}$ is the learned kernel generator for propagation view $\ell$
and $\rho_d,\rho_\theta$ denote the distance and orientation modulations. For
node features $\mathbf{h}_j$, the CLConv aggregation is
\begin{equation}
\operatorname{CLConv}(\mathbf{h})_{i}
=\sum_{\ell}\sum_{j\in\mathcal{N}(i)}
\mathbf{K}_{ij}^{(\ell)}\mathbf{h}_{j},
\label{eq:clconv_operator}
\end{equation}
with $\mathcal{N}(i)$ denoting the local spherical neighbourhood. In the
recurrent backbone, CLConv replaces the affine maps in the GRU gates:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\mathbf{r}_{t}&=\sigma\!\left(\operatorname{CLConv}_{r}([\mathbf{x}_{t}\Vert\mathbf{h}_{t-1}])\right),\\
\mathbf{u}_{t}&=\sigma\!\left(\operatorname{CLConv}_{u}([\mathbf{x}_{t}\Vert\mathbf{h}_{t-1}])\right),\\
\tilde{\mathbf{h}}_{t}&=\tanh\!\left(\operatorname{CLConv}_{c}([\mathbf{x}_{t}\Vert(\mathbf{r}_{t}\odot\mathbf{h}_{t-1})])\right),\\
\mathbf{h}_{t}&=\mathbf{u}_{t}\odot\mathbf{h}_{t-1}
 +(1-\mathbf{u}_{t})\odot\tilde{\mathbf{h}}_{t}.
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:clconv_gru}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Adaptive graph-fusion encoder}
The AGF encoder is applied to the embedded node features before the recurrent
backbone (Fig.~\ref{fig:overview}a; the graph construction is detailed in
Fig.~\ref{fig:overview}c). The per-node feature is formed by
concatenating a projected weather feature with a learned node embedding:
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{F}_{bti}
=\phi_x\!\left(\bar{\mathbf{x}}_{bti}\right)\Vert\mathbf{e}^{\mathrm{node}}_{i},
\qquad
\mathbf{F}\in\R^{B\times T\times N\times d}.
\label{eq:agf_input}
\end{equation}
Trainable source and destination embeddings
$\mathbf{E}_{s},\mathbf{E}_{t}\in\R^{N\times d_e}$ ($d_e=10$) define a directed
adjacency
\begin{equation}
A_{ij}
=\frac{\exp(\mathbf{e}^{s}_{i}{}^{\top}\mathbf{e}^{t}_{j})}
{\sum_{\ell=1}^{N}\exp(\mathbf{e}^{s}_{i}{}^{\top}\mathbf{e}^{t}_{\ell})},
\label{eq:adaptive_adjacency}
\end{equation}
equivalent to applying a row-wise softmax to
$\mathbf{E}_{s}\mathbf{E}_{t}^{\top}$. For each node the implementation retains
the self-edge and the $k-1$ largest remaining entries, then renormalizes the
selected weights:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{T}_{k}(i)=\{i\}\cup
\operatorname*{arg\,top}_{\substack{j\ne i\\ k-1}} A_{ij},\qquad
\tilde A_{ij}=\frac{A_{ij}}{\sum_{\ell\in\mathcal{T}_{k}(i)}A_{i\ell}},
\quad j\in\mathcal{T}_{k}(i).
\label{eq:topk_normalization}
\end{equation}
With $k=4$, the graph-mixed feature is
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{m}_{bti}=\sum_{j\in\mathcal{T}_k(i)}\tilde A_{ij}\,\mathbf{F}_{btj}.
\label{eq:graph_mixing}
\end{equation}
The graph-aggregated and residual features are projected separately,
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{q}_{bti}=\mathbf{W}_{m}\mathbf{m}_{bti}+\mathbf{b}_{m},\qquad
\mathbf{r}_{bti}=\mathbf{W}_{r}\mathbf{F}_{bti}+\mathbf{b}_{r},
\label{eq:agf_projections}
\end{equation}
and the learned gate is
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{g}_{bti}
=\sigma\!\big(\mathbf{W}_{g}[\mathbf{r}_{bti}\Vert\mathbf{q}_{bti}]
+\mathbf{b}_{g}\big).
\label{eq:agf_gate}
\end{equation}
The fused AGF output is
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{z}_{bti}
=\mathbf{g}_{bti}\odot\mathbf{q}_{bti}
+(1-\mathbf{g}_{bti})\odot\mathbf{r}_{bti}.
\label{eq:agf_fusion}
\end{equation}
The fused output $\mathbf{z}$ (dimension $8$) is concatenated with the standard
CLCRN encoder inputs before the unchanged recurrent encoder. The
schedule-matched control disables the entire AGF encoder, removing both the
adaptive top-$k$ mixing and its gate; the ablations in
Table~\ref{tab:ablation} replace the gate by the mean
$\tfrac12(\mathbf{q}_i+\mathbf{r}_i)$ (\emph{w/o gated fusion}) or disable the
adaptive branch (\emph{w/o adaptive graph}).

\subsection{Training configuration}
Both variants use two recurrent layers, 32 hidden units, an 8-dimensional
weather-feature embedding, an 8-dimensional CLConv embedding, one CLConv
propagation view, and curriculum learning in the autoregressive decoder.
CLCRN-AGF uses $d_e=10$-dimensional adaptive source/destination node
embeddings, an 8-dimensional AGF output, and $k=4$ selected neighbours
including the self-edge. Training uses
Adam with learning rate 0.01, batch size 32, gradient-norm clipping at 5,
and a MultiStepLR schedule with milestones
$\{10,20,30,40,50\}$ and multiplicative factor 0.95. Models train for at
most 100 epochs and the checkpoint with the lowest validation MAE is used for
test evaluation.

Experiments were run with Python 3.13, PyTorch 2.10, CUDA 12.8, and an NVIDIA
GeForce RTX 5090 Laptop GPU. Seeds 2021--2025 are applied to NumPy and all
PyTorch random-number generators. We do not claim bitwise deterministic
reproducibility across hardware or library versions.
Primary control and CLCRN-AGF runs were trained in standard precision. The
additional relative-humidity w/o gated fusion ablation in
Table~\ref{tab:ablation} was completed with PyTorch automatic mixed precision
and batch size 32 because standard-precision attempts repeatedly failed with
CUDA allocator exhaustion on the available Windows GPU.

\subsection{Metrics and statistical analysis}
MAE and RMSE are computed after inverse scaling. The corrected evaluator
includes all finite target values; zero is not treated as missing because zero
wind components and zero cloud cover are physically meaningful. Persistence
repeats the final input observation over the complete forecast horizon. The
primary analysis compares five seed replicates for each variant. For each
metric we report the mean and sample standard deviation across seeds, the mean
difference, a two-sided 95\% Welch confidence interval, and a two-sided Welch
test. All reported test rows use this zero-inclusive evaluator. For archived
runs with existing checkpoints, validation selection was recomputed with the
same corrected MAE among the saved checkpoints before test evaluation.
For $M$ finite target entries and seed-level metric values $m$, the reported
quantities are
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\mathrm{MAE}&=\frac{1}{M}\sum_{a=1}^{M}\left|\hat y_a-y_a\right|,
&\qquad
\mathrm{RMSE}&=\left(\frac{1}{M}\sum_{a=1}^{M}(\hat y_a-y_a)^2\right)^{1/2},\\
\Delta_m&=\bar m_{\mathrm{AGF}}-\bar m_{\mathrm{ctrl}},
&\qquad
t_W&=\frac{\bar m_{\mathrm{AGF}}-\bar m_{\mathrm{ctrl}}}
{\sqrt{s_{\mathrm{AGF}}^{2}/n_{\mathrm{AGF}}+s_{\mathrm{ctrl}}^{2}/n_{\mathrm{ctrl}}}}.
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:metrics_welch}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Computational complexity}
The original CLConv implementation materializes dense $N\times N$ kernel
operators, and the AGF encoder forms a dense $N\times N$ similarity matrix
$\mathbf{E}_s\mathbf{E}_t^{\top}$ before top-$k$ selection. The implementation
therefore has quadratic intermediate memory and similarity-computation cost in
$N$, despite the sparse aggregation that follows selection. Claims of linear
scaling are not made.
